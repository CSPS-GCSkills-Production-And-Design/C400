Lesson 1: The Driving Force for Response

Let’s begin with the driving force that provides authority and direction to the Government of Canada when responding to emergencies.

Underlying the Government of Canada’s roles and responsibilities in all four pillars of emergency management are the Emergency Management Act and the Federal Policy for Emergency Management.

Emergency Management Act

The Emergency Management Act sets out clear roles and responsibilities for all federal ministers across the full spectrum of emergency management.

With regard to response activities, the Act specifies that the Minister of Public Safety Canada is responsible for coordinating the Government of Canada’s response by:

  • monitoring potential imminent and actual emergencies and advising other ministers accordingly;
  • coordinating the Government of Canada’s response to an emergency;
  • coordinating the provision of financial and non-financial assistance to a province in the event of a provincial emergency;
  • facilitating the authorized sharing of information in order to enhance emergency management; and
  • coordinating Canada’s response to emergencies in the United States and providing assistance in response to those emergencies in consultation with the Minister of Global Affairs Canada (GAC).

The Act specifies that a government organization may not respond to a provincial emergency unless the government of the province requests assistance or if there is an agreement with the province that requires or permits the assistance.

Federal Policy for Emergency Management

The Federal Policy for Emergency Management was established under the authority of the Emergency Management Act and applies to all federal organizations.

In terms of response, the Federal Policy for Emergency Management directs federal organizations to:

  • respond to emergencies according to their response plans;
  • ensure their response is consistent with Public Safety Canada’s coordination of an integrated federal response;
  • align departmental plans with the Federal Emergency Response Plan;
  • support other federal organizations when requested;
  • undertake post-incident analysis for incorporating lessons learned and best practices into emergency management plans;
  • provide the Government Operations Centre with updated departmental response information; and
  • help ensure that trained and security-cleared staff liaison officers and subject matter experts are ready to be deployed to the Government Operations Centre in the event of an emergency.

Depending on the type of emergency, other federal legislative acts may be invoked including the:

  • Transportation of Dangerous Goods Act
  • National Defence Act
  • Energy Supplies Emergency Act

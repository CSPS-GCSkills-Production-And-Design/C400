Emergency Management Components

The following examples demonstrate the four interdependent and often overlapping components of emergency management.

These components are based on best practices in emergency management and research. They are recognized in many other countries, including Australia, the USA and Great Britain, as well as by The International Federation of Red Cross and Red Cross Societies around the world.

For a brief description, select each component of the EM continuum.

Prevention and Mitigation

To eliminate or reduce the risks associated with events or incidents in order to protect lives, property, the environment, and reduce economic disruption. Prevention/mitigation includes structural mitigative measures (e.g. construction of floodways and dykes) and non-structural mitigative measures (e.g. building codes, land-use planning, and insurance incentives). Prevention and mitigation may be considered independently or one may include the other.

Prepardness

To be ready to respond to a potential emergency and manage its consequences through measures taken prior to an event, such as emergency response plans, mutual assistance agreements, resource inventories and training, equipment and exercise programs.

Response

To act during or immediately after an emergency to manage its consequences through, for example, emergency public communication, search and rescue, emergency medical assistance and evacuation to minimize suffering and losses associated with disasters.

Recovery

To repair or restore conditions to an acceptable level through measures taken after an emergency, for example return of evacuees, trauma counseling, reconstruction, economic impact studies and financial assistance. There is a strong relationship between long-term sustainable recovery and prevention and mitigation of future emergencies. Recovery efforts should be conducted with a view towards disaster risk reduction.

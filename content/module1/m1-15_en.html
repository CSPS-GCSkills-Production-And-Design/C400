Purpose of Emergency Management:
  • save lives
  • preserve the environment
  • protect property and the economy

Emergency Management Components: Prevention/Mitigation

To eliminate or reduce the risks of major events and incidents in order to protect lives, property, the environment, and reduce economic disruption. Prevention/mitigation includes structural mitigative measures (e.g. construction of floodways and dykes) and non-structural mitigative measures (e.g. building codes, land-use planning, and insurance incentives). Prevention and mitigation may be considered independently or one may include the other.

Emergency Management Components: Preparedness

To be ready to respond to a major event or incident and manage its consequences through measures taken prior to an event, such as emergency response plans, business continuity plans, mutual assistance agreements, resource inventories and training, equipment and exercise programs.

Emergency Management Components: Response

To act during or immediately after a major event or incident to manage its consequences through, for example, emergency public communication, search and rescue, emergency medical assistance and evacuation to minimize suffering and losses associated with disasters.

Emergency Management Components: Recovery

To repair or restore conditions to an acceptable level through measures taken after a major event or incident, for example return of evacuees, trauma counseling, reconstruction, economic impact studies and financial assistance. There is a strong relationship between long-term sustainable recovery and prevention and mitigation of future disasters. Recovery efforts should be conducted with a view towards disaster risk reduction.

Emergency Management Organizations:

Provincial and territorial EMOs are the principle point of contact with the Government of Canada for emergency management issues, including request for support from municipal emergencies.

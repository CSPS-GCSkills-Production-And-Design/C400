EM in Action: Red River Floodway

Let’s turn to the history of the Red River Floodway for an example of emergency management in action.

In the spring of 1950, Winnipeg experienced a flood that would eventually lead to the construction of the Red River Floodway. During the flood, a state of emergency was declared and the Canadian Army was brought in to protect residents and property.

Over the course of the flood, 100,000 residents were evacuated from their homes and much of Winnipeg was inundated with floodwaters.

The water stayed above flood stage for 51 days, destroying approximately 10,000 homes and devastating hospitals, schools and businesses. The 1950 flood resulted in an estimated $125 million in damages (more than $1.3 billion in today’s figures).

After reviewing the impact of the flood, Manitoba’s Premier, Duff Roblin, sparked the idea of constructing a diversion channel around Winnipeg that could be used during extreme flood emergencies. At the time, there were many people who questioned the need for the floodway.

In 1959, Roblin, who was the leader of a minority government, put his political career on the line by making the floodway the key issue of the provincial election. That year, Roblin won the election and received a mandate to build the floodway.

The Red River Floodway was built and completed in 1968 at a total cost of $63 million with the federal government contributing 60% of the funding to support the project.

At the time, the project was the second largest earthmoving project in the world – next only to the construction of the Panama Canal.

Since its construction, the Red River Floodway has helped reduce the impact of subsequent floods (1997, 2009, and 2011), and has saved Manitoba more than $32 billion in flood damage, including an estimated $6 billion in potential damage and recovery costs related to the 1997 “Flood of the Century”. In 2014, the Manitoba Floodway Authority completed a $665 million expansion project of the Red River Floodway, cost-shared equally by the Government of Canada and the Province of Manitoba, to increase capacity of the Floodway to provide protection from a flood magnitude that was estimated to only be exceeded once in 700 years.
